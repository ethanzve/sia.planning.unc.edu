For nearly a century, visitors came to Moscow\u2019s Tretyakov Gallery to admire the perfect harmony of Russia\u2019s most famous icon: the \u201cTrinity\u201d, painted by Andrei Rublev in the Middle Ages.<\/p>\n

The almost 600-year-old artwork depicting three angels is one of the most recognisable Russian masterpieces in the world.<\/p>\n

Last month, however, President Vladimir Putin handed over the historic icon to the Russian Orthodox Church, the latest sign of the tightening alliance between the Kremlin and religious leaders.<\/p>\n

The handover has sparked an outcry from restorers and art historians, who warn the extremely fragile mediaeval icon might not survive outside the Tretyakov Gallery\u2019s walls.<\/p>\n

It comes as Russia\u2019s offensive in Ukraine stretches into its second year, with Patriarch Kirill throwing his support behind the assault and saying that dying in Ukraine \u201cwashes away all sins\u201d.<\/p>\n

Lev Lifshits, one of the country\u2019s leading art historians, warned that the \u201cTrinity\u201d could be destroyed and said he believed the decision to give it to the church was political.<\/p>\n

He compared its state to an ailing person.<\/p>\n

\u201cIf you suddenly take a critically ill person out of an intensive care unit, what do you think would happen?\u201d<\/p>\n

Some political observers say Putin\u2019s move is a spiritual decision dictated by the difficult situation on the front line in Ukraine.<\/p>\n

\u201cStill no victory,\u201d said political analyst Georgy Bovt. \u201cAll that remains is for Putin to ask God for help.\u201d<\/p>\n

With Moscow\u2019s military campaign in Ukraine stalling, Russian authorities have been increasingly willing to depict the offensive in religious terms.<\/p>\n

The masterpiece was painted for what is now the Trinity Lavra of St. Sergius located in the town of Sergiyev Posad outside Moscow.<\/p>\n

After the 1917 Bolshevik revolution, the Soviet authorities transferred the artwork to the Tretyakov Gallery in 1929.<\/p>\n

The church said the icon would be first exhibited at the Christ the Saviour Cathedral in Moscow before returning to the historic monastery in Sergiyev Posad. Religious leaders insist they have every means at their disposal to preserve the precious icon.<\/p>\n

It is the second transfer of a national treasure to the church in recent weeks.<\/p>\n

The Saint Petersburg-based Hermitage Museum said another Russian monastery would receive the silver sarcophagus of Alexander Nevsky, a medieval prince and national hero.<\/p>\n

Hermitage director Mikhail Piotrovsky, who has lauded the Ukraine offensive, said it was the right move \u201cat this geopolitical time\u201d.<\/p>\n

\u201cToday, the sacred signficance of the monument is more important than its artistic value,\u201d he told reporters.<\/p>\n

\u2018Helped Russian princes\u2019<\/p>\n

In a sign the church will not tolerate dissent, Patriarch Kirill has fired and banned from the priesthood a cleric heading the Moscow Patriarchate\u2019s expert council on church art, who said that the safety of the icon was paramount.<\/p>\n

Speaking to AFP before his dismissal, Archpriest Leonid Kalinin said the icon\u2019s \u201crightful place is where it was painted to be\u201d.<\/p>\n

Standing in an ornate Moscow church, the cleric welcomed the fact that Putin, a former KGB spy, \u201cturns to shrines in difficult and fateful moments\u201d.<\/p>\n

By giving the icon back to the church, Putin wants to \u201creturn Russia to its core\u201d, he added.<\/p>\n

Putin has sought to depict the offensive in Ukraine as a battle against the decadent West, and used religious symbols to rouse support.<\/p>\n

The Kremlin said that during a rare visit to occupied southern Ukraine in April, the Russian president gave troops a copy of a historic icon.<\/p>\n

Art historian Lifshits said the \u201cTrinity\u201d had helped Russian rulers in difficult times.<\/p>\n

\u201cContemplating Rublev\u2019s \u2018Trinity\u2019 helped Russian princes overcome feuds,\u201d he said.<\/p>\n

Popular legend has it that Joseph Stalin, the ruthless dictator of the atheist Soviet Union, also sought divine help in the country\u2019s epic battle against Nazi Germany.<\/p>\n

According to one popular myth, in a bid to save Moscow from approaching German troops in 1941, a plane carrying an icon flew over the Russian capital. Two years later Stalin met religious leaders and blessed cooperation between the church and the Soviet government.<\/p>\n

\u2018Unjustified risks\u2019<\/p>\n

Rublev\u2019s most famous work has left the Tretyakov Gallery only a handful of times, including during World War II when it was evacuated to safety.<\/p>\n

In 2022, the icon travelled back to the Trinity Lavra of St. Sergius for religious celebrations.<\/p>\n

Art experts said the piece suffered significant damage when it was temporarily moved.<\/p>\n

Lilia Yevseyeva, an art historian at the Museum of Russian Icons in Moscow, said that, if the icon leaves the Tretyakov Gallery for good, \u201cfuture generations will not see it in its current state\u201d.<\/p>\n

Following the public outcry, Putin\u2019s spokesman Dmitry Peskov said the icon would be exhibited at the Christ the Saviour Cathedral, where experts will decide on its future restoration.<\/p>\n

Art experts are adamant the piece should not leave the museum.<\/p>\n

Even members of the Russian Academy of Sciences broke their silence, saying the icon cannot be moved, \u201ceven for a short period of time\u201d.<\/p>\n

In an open letter to the culture minister they said only a museum could ensure the preservation of Rublev\u2019s \u201cTrinity\u201d.<\/p>\n
